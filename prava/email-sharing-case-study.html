Email Sharing Redesign

From technical permissions to people-first sharing

IceWarp Email Client • 2011 • UX Research & Design

Problem

Users struggle with technical permission dialogs

The original email client used technical permission settings (A, R, W, M, D checkboxes) that forced users to think about system architecture rather than their actual goal: sharing folders with specific people.

Original: Technical permission matrix with A, R, W, M, D checkboxes

What was wrong

  • Technical acronyms (A, R, W, M, D)
  • Separate permissions for Items vs Folder
  • Focus on system settings, not user goals
  • Contact picker as separate step
  • No context about frequently shared contacts

User's actual need

  • Quick search for people by name
  • See frequently contacted people
  • Visual, card-based selection
  • Simple sharing actions
  • Clear indication of who has access

Key Insight

Users don't think "I need to set Read/Write permissions on this folder." They think "I need to share this with Jan and Franta." The interface should match their mental model.

Solution

People-first sharing interface

Instead of starting with technical permissions, the redesign focuses on the real user problem: finding the right people to share with.

Design Process

From sketches to solution

Early paper prototypes explored different approaches to people-first sharing, focusing on search, recommendations, and visual selection.

Approach

Design principles

1. Match mental models

Users think in terms of people, not permissions. The interface should reflect how they naturally think about sharing.

2. Reduce cognitive load

Instead of making users remember what "A, R, W, M, D" means, show visual cards with clear actions.

3. Leverage context

Show recommendations based on frequently contacted people - most users share with the same people repeatedly.

4. Search-first approach

Make search the primary interaction - users know who they want to share with, just help them find those people quickly.
Impact

Expected outcomes

By focusing on the real user problem (finding people to share with) rather than technical implementation (permission settings), the redesign makes sharing intuitive and fast.

Key Takeaway

Solve the real problem, not the technical one

This project taught me to always question whether the interface reflects the user's mental model or the system's architecture. Technical accuracy doesn't equal usability. The best solution is often the one that completely reframes the problem from the user's perspective.
