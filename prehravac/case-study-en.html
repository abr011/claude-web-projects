When the Designer Is the User

The Problem

Five Screens to See One Book

I had 2,000 audiobooks on Audioteka. To see what a single book is about, I had to scroll through five screens: a giant cover consuming the entire viewport, redundant metadata, a collapsed description prefixed with boilerplate ("Audiokniha [title], autor [author], cte [narrator], rezie [director]"), and when I finally expanded it — marketing copy, CAPSLOCK taglines, and user reviews. The actual story description was buried four screens down.

One book. Five screens. The actual story description starts on screen 3.
5 screens for 1 book

The library wasn't better. 119 favorites in a flat list — cover, author, title, rating. No search by narrator. No genre filtering. No series grouping. No way to find anything except scrolling.

The player showed a giant cover consuming 60% of the screen. Chapter names like "19_Muz_na_stezce_13" with no book-level time. Sleep timer stopped the book but couldn't tell me where I fell asleep.

Even the AI said I shouldn't start this project. "Use an existing platform." But I didn't like the players.

The AI Approach

Turning Marketing Noise into Useful Metadata

Audioteka's book descriptions are marketing copy. They repeat the author and narrator (already shown in metadata), add the director's name (nobody cares), prefix everything with "Audiokniha [title] obsahuje...", and pad the end with CAPSLOCK taglines and credit blocks.

Instead of displaying this raw text, I built a pipeline: descriptions are cleaned by stripping boilerplate prefixes, CAPSLOCK lines, credit blocks, and redundant series tags. Then Google Gemini reads the cleaned text and generates genre tags (from a taxonomy of 9 genres and 28 sub-genres), detects series membership, and writes short series descriptions in Czech.

What Bothered Me Most

The Details That Nobody Fixes

  • Players play chapters — so the progress bar sometimes represents 8 minutes, sometimes 3 hours. I don't care about chapters. I care how much of the book is left.
  • No way to filter by narrator — but half the reason I pick a book is who reads it
  • Sleep timer stops the book. Great. Now I wake up and can't rewind to where I fell asleep — because chapters.
  • Offline is a manual chore: download before, delete after. Forget either and you're stuck.
  • Descriptions are marketing copy — credits, taglines, boilerplate. The actual story is buried.
  • No series awareness — "Part 3 of 7" nowhere to be found. Which book comes next? Guess.

What I Changed

Built for How I Actually Listen

  • Book time, not chapter time — elapsed and remaining for the entire book. The progress bar shows ±30 minutes around where you are.
  • Narrator is first-class — tap any narrator name to filter the library. Because sometimes the voice matters more than who wrote it.
  • Sleep timer with rewind — set 30 minutes, fall asleep, wake up. The player offers to rewind exactly those 30 minutes. One tap.
  • Offline just works — auto-prefetches the next 30 minutes during playback. Auto-deletes old cache when storage fills up. No manual downloads, no cleanup.
  • AI-cleaned descriptions — boilerplate stripped, marketing removed, cut at 800 characters. Genre and sub-genre tags generated by Gemini. Story first, credits never.
  • Series make sense — books grouped into series cards with position ("Part 3 of 7"). AI-written series descriptions instead of copy-pasted marketing blurbs.

What I Learned Building This

I'm a UX designer, not a developer. I built this with AI because I wanted to see what's possible — and because I needed a real project to develop a UX design skill beyond theory. Not my skill — Claude's. Every design decision I made became a documented principle: "names are navigation," "show enough to decide without tapping," "system handles errors, user does nothing." The skill grew from a generic design system to 24KB of battle-tested patterns in five days. Now Claude applies them to new projects automatically.

When you're both the designer and the user, you can test a control layout at 11pm and change it by midnight. The skip buttons changed three times. The sleep timer was preset to 30 minutes from day one because that's what I always set it to. No speed options — I never use them. Every decision was filtered through one question: does this help me listen to a book in bed?

The AI text pipeline was an afterthought that became the backbone. Once every book had clean descriptions and genre tags, the entire library became browsable. What started as "fix the player" turned into "rethink how audiobook metadata should work." 2,000 books, all retagged, all with cleaned descriptions, all filterable by narrator, genre, and series — computed from the same raw data that Audioteka buries under marketing copy.
